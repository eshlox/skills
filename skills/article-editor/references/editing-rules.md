# Editing Rules by Concern (with sources and reasoning)

Distilled from a century of converging advice: Strunk & White (The Elements of Style), Zinsser (On Writing Well), Orwell (Politics and the English Language), Stephen King (On Writing), Pinker (The Sense of Style), Roy Peter Clark (Writing Tools), Ann Handley (Everybody Writes), Heath brothers (Made to Stick), Ogilvy, Paul Graham ("Write Simply"), Scott Adams, Julian Shapiro (Writing Well Handbook), Nielsen Norman Group web-reading research, and plainlanguage.gov. These sources independently agree on the core, which is why the rules below are treated as settled practice rather than taste.

## 1. Simplicity and plain language (Orwell, Graham, plainlanguage.gov, Fowler)

- Never use a long word where a short one will do. Prefer the familiar to the far-fetched, the concrete to the abstract, the single word to the circumlocution.
- Simple writing travels: it reads faster, persuades better ("a good argument in five sentences will sway more people than a brilliant argument in a hundred" — Adams), and is kinder to non-native readers. The less energy readers spend on the prose, the more they have for the ideas (Graham).
- Plain does not mean dumbed-down (plainlanguage.gov). Keep the sophistication of the ideas; simplify only words and sentences. Pinker's "classic style": prose as a window onto the world, writer and reader as equals.
- Cut jargon and idioms or explain them — idioms especially trip non-native readers (NN/g).
- One idea per sentence. Short sentences by default; long ones only when controlled and earning their length.

## 2. Conciseness (Strunk & White, Orwell, Clark, Pinker)

- "Omit needless words. Vigorous writing is concise" (Strunk). If a word can be cut, cut it (Orwell).
- Standard cuts: "in order to"→"to"; "due to the fact that"→"because"; "at this point in time"→"now"; "close proximity"→"proximity"; intensifiers ("very", "really", "quite", "actually") usually delete.
- Cut big, then small (Clark): first remove passages that don't serve the focus, then tighten words.
- Delete metadiscourse — writing about the writing: "In this article I will...", "As mentioned above" (Pinker).

## 3. Sentence mechanics (Clark, King, Pinker, plainlanguage.gov)

- Begin sentences with subject and verb; make meaning early, let weaker elements branch right (Clark). Keep subject, verb, and object close together.
- Active voice by default. "The passive voice is safe" — which is why timid writing overuses it (King). Passive is fine when it correctly directs attention to the receiver of the action (Pinker).
- Activate verbs; replace nominalizations (-ance, -ment, -ation, -ing nouns) with the verb inside them.
- Adverb test (Clark/King): a good adverb changes the verb's meaning ("smiled sadly"); a bad one repeats it ("completely destroyed"). Cut the bad ones.
- 2-3-1 emphasis (Clark): strongest element at the end of a sentence or paragraph, next strongest at the start, weakest in the middle.
- Word territory (Clark): don't repeat a distinctive word within a small space unless intentional.
- Present tense where possible; consistent tense and terminology throughout.

## 4. Structure and flow (NN/g, Pinker, Clark, Handley)

- Web readers scan: 79% scan, 16% read word-by-word (Nielsen 1997). Concise + scannable + objective writing measured 124% better usability combined. Structure is not decoration.
- Inverted pyramid: conclusion first, support after. Front-load the point of the article, of each section, of each paragraph.
- One idea per paragraph; the key sentence first (scanners skip the rest).
- Given-before-new (Pinker): open each sentence/paragraph with information the reader already has, then add the new. This is what "flow" mechanically is.
- Group related content; check the section order tells one clear progression.
- Transitions show relationships between ideas — but natural ones ("that's why", "but", "so", "on the other hand"), not mechanical connectors.
- Establish a pattern, then twist it (Clark): pattern creates comfort, the twist creates attention.

## 5. Headings and titles (Ogilvy, NN/g, Handley)

- Five times as many people read the headline as the body; "when you have written your headline, you have spent eighty cents out of your dollar" (Ogilvy).
- Clear beats clever. No puns, no wordplay, no faddish vocabulary — they slow comprehension, confuse non-native speakers, and cost credibility (Ogilvy, NN/g).
- Specific beats vague: not "Make better decisions with data" but "Data visualization helps you detect fraud faster" (NN/g).
- Each heading must work out of context (readers land mid-page, screen readers list headings).
- Front-load the strongest keyword — readers weight the first two words of a heading most (NN/g).
- Omit nonessential words. Length itself is not the sin; vagueness is ("if you need a long headline, go ahead" — Ogilvy).
- Titles: promise what the piece delivers; open a genuine curiosity gap without clickbait (Handley); numbers and specifics help; draft several versions and pick (Ogilvy wrote 20+).
- Meaningful subheadings and occasional lists make a piece scannable — but over-bulleting and over-bolding are AI tells; prose should still carry the piece.

## 6. Engagement (Shapiro, Heath, Zinsser, Clark)

- Hook first: a half-told story, bold claim, or curiosity gap in the opening buys goodwill for the whole piece (Shapiro). The first sentence should create curiosity (Adams).
- Concrete and sensory beats abstract (Heath, Made to Stick: Simple, Unexpected, Concrete, Credible, Emotional, Stories). Individual examples beat categories.
- Disperse novelty: find flat stretches with no insight or delight and either add one (from the author's material) or cut the stretch (Shapiro's "dopamine counting").
- Vary rhythm — sentence lengths, paragraph lengths. Monotony bores regardless of content.
- Voice is the author's unfiltered self on paper (Shapiro, Zinsser). Writing that sounds like a person "stands out as a real person among the robots" (Zinsser). Protect it above all.
- Read the draft aloud (or simulate it): stumbles and breath-runs mark sentences to fix (Zinsser, Handley).

## 7. Light-touch editing discipline (professional line-editing practice)

- Line editing improves clarity, flow, and rhythm at sentence level while preserving voice; it is not developmental editing (restructuring) and not rewriting.
- The professional test: if the finished piece doesn't sound like the author wrote it, the editor failed.
- Query, don't overwrite, anything that might be deliberate: fragments, unusual words, informal register, humor.
- Rewrite the minimum necessary. Every edit must earn its place; a fine sentence stays untouched.
- Keep every fact, number, citation, and claim exactly as the author had it.
